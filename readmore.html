Influence-based Pathfinding

Influence-based pathfinding is an algorithm I developed in my research, alongside a team of six under the Rutgers Aresty Research Program. It's created by combining the techniques of hierarchical pathfinding, A*, and influence maps.

The situation: We wanted to create a pathfinding algorithm that is better applicable to human environments. The problem with current pathfinding algorithms is that they're static and generally based on finding the shortest path from a start to a goal. Human environments operate in opposing ways. Our environment is always changing due to traffic and infrastructure, and we travel based on how we're feeling. Maybe we want to avoid crowds? Maybe we want to take the scenic route?

My job was to implement the HPA* algorithm and make it more dynamic. What's cool about HPA* is that it's best used for large environments, much like our own world. After that, we added influence maps to replicate human feelings and stimuli.

The results: The result was a working demo that simulates humans that avoids crowd congestion. Here's a screenshot of that demo below, with the influence maps visible in many colors, agents in dark blue, and the path chosen in cyan. I even got to present these results at a poster session for my program.

Overall, this was an interesting experience, since this was the first time I got to contribute something new to the field in a professional setting. If I were to revisit this project, I would definitely fix the efficiency of the project!

Shoot 'em Up Game: notes on crying

This short video game was the first video game I've ever created and deployed! Like my Untitled Bullet Hell Game, this is another homage to the bullet hell genre, but in small doses to explore mental health in video games.

The situation: Mental health in video games is one of my favorite topics. As someone struggling with my own mental health issues, I care deeply about opening the dialogue about it. I find that one of the best ways I can contribute to that movement is by making games that addresses it. Video games are an interesting medium such that they're interactive, unlike most media like movies or visuals. We can present a stronger message to the audience because of this interactivity.

Since this was my first game, I attempted to make it as quickly as possible. This way, I can learn the most about video game development without overwhelming myself with work. The game features a little character, who is crying alone. The player can deal with this situation in various ways, such as crying it out, or reaching out to someone. My hope is that players get to learn more about how people deal with their mental health. Hence a small "note on crying."

The results: I ultimately finished this game within 4 days. It's a very short game, and can be finished within minutes. This was quite hectic though! Everything is basically held by sticks and strings. I learned the importance of project planning however. If I were to redo this game, then I would spend more time planning it. Now, project planning is one of my favorite steps of my projects! I hope to make more games about mental health in the future.

Android Chess

This was an academic project developed in a pair. The goal was to make a clone of chess, but as an Android app.

The situation: This project was assigned as we were learning about design patterns in OOP. So, the full task was to make a clone of chess, but also used what we've learned to make the app more efficient for the user and the programmer. Another interesting feature we were tasked to add was to make a replay feature. This way, players can save their game and review the moves that they made during the game.

The results: Just as you expect, a working app - simple, but functional! I found that working with Android and Android Studio was actually easy The UI and serialization in Android are very similar to those in Java, a language I'm fluent in. Also, we didn't find the need to implement a lot of design patterns, If I were to revisit this project though, I would still attempt to implement them regardless. This is because, at the very least, all screens can return to previous screens and can exit the app. That kind of functionality, though small, can still be shared efficiently. I would also added some polish to make the game more exciting, such as by including animations. I'm a indie game developer after all! I'd love to work more with apps in Android!
